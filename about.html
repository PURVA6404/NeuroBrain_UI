Understanding Brain Tumors

Essential information about brain tumors, their types, and characteristics

What is a Brain Tumor?

A brain tumor is an abnormal mass of tissue in which cells grow and multiply uncontrollably. These growths can be benign (non-cancerous) or malignant (cancerous).

Classification

Brain tumors are classified into primary (originating in the brain) and secondary (spreading from elsewhere in the body). There are more than 120 different types identified by medical science.

Grading System

The World Health Organization (WHO) grades brain tumors from I to IV. Grade I tumors are least aggressive, while Grade IV are the most aggressive and fast-growing.

WHO Grading Scale

Grade I

Slow-growing cells that look nearly normal and are generally associated with long-term survival. Usually benign and can often be completely removed with surgery.

Grade II

Relatively slow-growing cells with a slightly abnormal appearance. These tumors can sometimes invade nearby tissues and may recur as higher-grade tumors.

Grade III

Actively reproducing abnormal cells that infiltrate adjacent normal brain tissue. These tumors tend to recur, often as a higher grade.

Grade IV

Rapidly reproducing abnormal cells that form new blood vessels to maintain growth. These are the most aggressive and life-threatening tumors.

Common Brain Tumor Types

Gliomas

The most common type of primary brain tumors, originating from glial cells that surround and support nerve cells.

Subtypes:
  • Astrocytomas: Arising from star-shaped cells called astrocytes, including glioblastoma multiforme (GBM), the most aggressive form.
  • Oligodendrogliomas: Derived from cells that make myelin, the insulation for brain wiring.
  • Ependymomas: Formed from cells lining the ventricles and spinal cord central canal.

Prevalence: Account for approximately 33% of all brain tumors.

Meningiomas

The most common benign intracranial tumors, developing in the meninges, the layers of tissue covering the brain and spinal cord.

Characteristics:

  • About 85% are non-cancerous, slow-growing tumors
  • More common in women than men
  • Typically found in adults over 40
  • May grow quite large before causing symptoms

Prevalence: Account for about 30% of all brain tumors.

Pituitary Adenomas

Benign tumors that grow from the pituitary gland at the base of the brain, often affecting hormone production.

Characteristics:

  • Can cause hormonal imbalances throughout the body
  • May affect vision due to proximity to optic nerves
  • Generally treatable with surgery and/or medication
  • Rarely become malignant

Prevalence: Account for about 10% of all brain tumors.

Signs and Symptoms

Brain tumor symptoms vary depending on the tumor's size, type, and location in the brain. Symptoms may develop gradually or appear suddenly.

Treatment Approaches

Treatment for brain tumors depends on several factors, including the tumor type, grade, location, and the patient's overall health.

Surgery

The primary treatment for most brain tumors, aiming to remove as much of the tumor as possible without damaging critical brain tissue.

Advanced techniques include:

  • Computer-assisted navigation
  • Intraoperative MRI
  • Awake craniotomy
  • Minimally invasive approaches

Radiation Therapy

Uses high-energy rays to kill tumor cells or stop them from growing. Often used after surgery or for tumors that cannot be surgically removed.

Types include:

  • External beam radiation
  • Stereotactic radiosurgery (Gamma Knife, CyberKnife)
  • Proton therapy
  • Brachytherapy

Chemotherapy

Uses drugs to kill cancer cells throughout the body. May be given orally, intravenously, or placed directly in the tumor cavity during surgery.

Administration methods:

  • Systemic chemotherapy
  • Intra-arterial chemotherapy
  • Wafer implants (Gliadel)
  • Blood-brain barrier disruption

Innovative Approaches

Emerging treatments are showing promise in clinical trials and specialized centers.

New therapies include:

  • Targeted therapy (targeting specific gene mutations)
  • Immunotherapy (helping the immune system fight cancer)
  • Tumor Treating Fields (TTFields)
  • Gene therapy
